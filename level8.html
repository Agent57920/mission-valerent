Scene: Cafeteria
The bell rang, and students rushed toward the cafeteria in a chaotic wave of noise and laughter.
Avron did not rush.
He walked slowly from table to table, scanning for a seat. It wasn’t that the cafeteria was full. In fact, it was emptier than usual.
But every time he approached an empty seat, someone would casually spit on it or wipe it with a dirty napkin, making it unusable.
No one wanted to sit with Avron.
Avron had Progeria. His physical appearance made him an easy target. Students avoided him. Some mocked him. Others simply pretended he didn’t exist.
Even teachers ignored him.
Next
Whenever he raised his hand in class, their eyes passed over him. They called on someone else. If he won a prize in a competition, his name was often replaced with another student’s name on the final announcement. He had been denied more than twenty awards he rightfully earned.
He never protested.
He was too shy to question authority. And since no one else cared, the pattern continued.
Once, he had been selected to participate in a school sports event. It was his first time being part of something public. He arrived excited that morning — only to find his name replaced by a student close to the principal.
That was the day he stopped expecting fairness.
Years passed.
Eventually, he became the head editor of the school newspaper — not because his talent was recognized, but because no one else wanted the position. Students believed the newspaper club was for “losers.”
Next
Avron accepted it quietly.
It was something.
Time: Valentine’s Week
On February 7th, Rose Day, Avron was assigned to design a special edition of the newspaper.
He turned it into a puzzle.
He hid repeated words inside an article and pinned it to the school notice board.
He did not expect anyone to read it.
No one ever did.
But that day, a girl stopped.
She read every line carefully.
Next
Then she circled the repeated words — every single “rose” — and beneath them she wrote:
“Make it a little harder next time. – Tanice.”
Avron stood at a distance, stunned.
When he approached the board, his heart raced.
She had solved it.
That night, he searched the school records for her name.
He found everything he could — class, section, academic record.
He debated all night whether he should approach her.
The next day at lunch, nervous but determined, he walked up to her.
“H–Hi.”
Next
She smiled politely.
“Do I know you?”
“Not yet. I’m the editor of the school newspaper.”
“Oh. The puzzle guy.”
He almost smiled.
“Yes. The puzzle guy.”
“It was good,” she said. “But you can do better.”
The bell rang.
“I have to go,” she said. “Meet me at Downtown Café tomorrow after school?”
She handed him a page with her number written on the back.
For the first time in his life, Avron felt noticed.
Next
Time: Chocolate Day
He prepared a harder puzzle.
Fourteen chocolates — green, yellow, white, and blue wrappers. A note:
“One of them has a golden ticket. Find it.”
Tanice found the box in her locker.
She took it to the restroom.
When she returned, she left a box of thirteen chocolates near Avron’s locker.
All wet.
The missing one?
The one with the golden ticket.
Inside the box was a note:
“Don’t be late.”
Next
At the café, Avron asked how she solved it.
She explained calmly that most chocolates would float in water due to density, but the one with the ticket would sink because of extra weight.
He didn’t hear a word.
He was too busy staring at her.
Time: After Six Months
They became friends.
Real friends.
They went to cafés, arcades, malls, parks.
She teased him. He laughed more.
For the first time, he had someone to sit with during lunch.
Teachers stopped skipping his hand. Students stopped mocking him openly.
Not because they changed.
But because he was no longer alone.
Next
Six months passed like this.
Stable. Happy.
He began to feel something deeper.
But neither of them named it.
Scene: The Rope
One afternoon, on a bridge near school, he handed her a rope.
“Hold this.”
She rolled her eyes but held one end.
Minutes passed.
Wind pulled between them.
She grew irritated.
Next
“This is ridiculous.”
“Promise me we won’t drift apart,” he said quietly.
She didn’t promise.
She just didn’t let go.
“You don’t have to promise. I believe you.”
But that night, he couldn’t sleep.
He saw a couple’s shadow outside his window — hugging.
The power of that hug was so strong that every problem of the world was very weak in front of that
He realized what he felt.
This was the same feeling he felt with Tanice
It wasn’t friendship.
Next
It was love.
He wrote everything in a letter and placed it under a vase with a red rose.
Tomorrow, he would confess.
Scene: The Disappearance
She didn’t come to school the next day.
He assumed she was sick.
After three days, he asked her classmates.
They laughed at him.
Teachers ignored his questions.
A week passed.
She was gone.
Next
No explanation.
No goodbye.
The cafeteria returned to how it used to be.
He sat alone again.
Mockery resumed.
He broke.
He cried randomly. Laughed randomly.
His parents took him to a psychiatrist.
He resisted at first, then told her everything — about the bullying, the newsletter, Tanice.
Avron sat at his study table, staring at the wilted red rose inside the white vase.
His room felt suffocating.
Next
Suddenly, his phone lit up.
A notification appeared on the lock screen.
For a brief second, the display picture of him and Tanice filled the screen.
The date read: March 10.
His chest tightened.
In anger, he switched off the phone and pushed it away.
Before he could process anything further—
A loud crash shattered the silence.
Glass burst inward.
A blue teddy bear, tied to a small stone, slammed onto his floor.
His heart stopped.
Next
Attached to the teddy was a golden token.
His fingers trembled as he turned it over.
I’m back.”
He didn’t think.
He ran downstairs.
And there she was.
Tanice.
Standing in the hallway.
Real.
Breathing.
Wearing a black dress.
Next
Pinned to it was a small metallic badge: 14.
Avron broke down instantly.
He ran to her and hugged her tightly, crying uncontrollably.
You disappeared,” he whispered. “Why did you disappear?”
She did not answer immediately.
Instead, she placed her hand gently on his back.
“I’m here now.”
They sat at the dining table.
Avron kept staring at her as if she might vanish again.
“Where were you?” he demanded softly. “Do you know what happened to me?”
Next
She smiled faintly.
“Five days left,” she said. “Then everything ends.”
“What ends?” he asked, confused. “And why are you wearing 14?”
She looked at him, expression unreadable.
“Ask no questions. Hear no lies.”
At that exact moment—
The front door opened.
Avron’s parents walked in, followed by the psychiatrist.
Avron froze
The psychiatrist’s eyes were fixed on him.
“Avron,” she said gently, “who are you talking to?”
Next
He turned slowly toward Tanice.
She was still sitting there.
Looking at him.
Smiling.
The psychiatrist continued speaking to his parents.
“I have confirmed with the school administration. There has never been a student named Tanice enrolled there. Not in the past. Not currently.”
Avron’s breathing became uneven.
“That’s not true,” he said immediately. “She’s right here.”
The psychiatrist stepped closer.
“Avron… no one else can see her.”
His parents looked terrified.
Next
The psychiatrist continued calmly:
“When a person experiences prolonged social isolation and bullying, the brain can create a protective construct — a hallucinated figure — to prevent psychological collapse. That figure feels real. It interacts. It evolves.”
Avron shook his head.
“No.”
She continued:
“In most documented cases, the hallucinated figure appears when hope is fading. And often… it pushes the person toward self-destruction.”
Avron slowly turned back toward Tanice.
She was still there.
Still calm.
Still smiling.
Next
“Five days,” she repeated softly.
He stared at the pin on her dress.
“What happens in five days?” he asked.
She leaned closer.
“Everything ends.”
His hands began to shake.
He suddenly remembered the letter.
The letter he had written months ago.
He ran upstairs, grabbed it from beneath the vase, and rushed back down.
Next
Tanice was standing near the doorway now.
He grabbed her hand.
Tears filled his eyes.
“I was going to tell you something that day,” he said.
She tilted her head slightly.
“Then tell me now.”
He unfolded the letter.
His voice trembled.
“Will you be my—”
THE END
